\section{The $\matilda$ Calculus - Dialectical Variant}
  \cite{Griffin1990-FormulaeAsTypesOfControl, Parigot1992-LambdaMu, Wadler2003-CBVIsDualToCBN} and \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} extend the proposition-as-types correspondence to classical logic. Griffin showed that \emph{control operators} such as call-cc can be typed by Peirce's law; Parigot's $\lambda\mu$-calculus provides the Curry-Howard-Correspondent to classical natural deduction; \cite{Wadler2003-CBVIsDualToCBN}, \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} invented symmetric systems, in the latter case the \matilda calculus which is to the classical sequent calculus LK as $\lambda\mu$-calculus is to classical natural deduction.

  The latter system can be transposed into a dialectical natural deduction system \cite{Herbelin2005CestMaintenant}, \cite{LovasCrary2006StructuralNormalization} \cite{Harper2016PFPL} of \emph{proofs and refutations}, providing us with the second one of our requirements.

We recap the $\matilda$ Calculus and give a dialectical variant of its syntax.
Expressions are divided into terms, environments and commands.
Terms are read left-to-right while environments are read right-to-left.
Commands are read either left-to-right or right-to-left depending on how they are bound.
The terminology on the expression level gestures at an interpretation of $\matilda$-expressions as program terms and continuation contexts.
Terms are expressions with outer holes that wait to be passed to a context.
Contexts are expressions with inner holes that await a term to be passed to them.
Commands assign terms to contexts.
The $\mu$ binder when binding to the right represents a call-with-current-continuation.
Symmetrically, when binding to the left $\mu$ corresponds to a call-with-current-expression.
We say that a $\mu$-bound variable is \emph{affine}  if it does not appear free in the command in which it is bound (likewise we call a binder binding an affine variable an \emph{affine binder}).
If we want to explicitly indicate that a binder is affine we write $\mu\_.c$ and $c.\_\mu$, respectively.
Two fragments of the syntax are of interest:
\emph{Intuitionistic Logic} is obtained by restricting the domain of the environment-variables $\alpha$ to a singleton set. We use $\star$ for this unique continuation variable whenever we want to work in the intuitionistic fragment.
Conversely, \emph{Cointuitionistic Logic} is obtained by restricting the term variable domain to a singleton. We use $\cdot$ to denote this unique term variable whenever we are wokring in the cointuitionistic fragment.


  \begin{grammar}[Grammar of the $\matilda$-calculus][tbhp][gram:terms]
    \firstcasesubtil{$v$}{x \gralt \lambda x:\sigma.v \gralt E \circ v \gralt \mu \sigma:\alpha.c}{Terms}

    \firstcasesubtil{$E$}{\alpha \gralt E.\sigma:\alpha\lambda \gralt v \circ E \gralt c.x:\sigma \mu}{Environments}

    \firstcasesubtil{$c$}{\rangle v :\sigma: E \langle}{Commands}

    \firstcasesubtil{$\sigma,\delta$}{a \gralt \sigma \rightarrow \delta \gralt \sigma - \delta}{Propositions}
  \end{grammar}

Sequents have the form \emph{Antecedent $\vdash$ Stoup $\dashv$ Succedent}.
Antecedent and Succedent are conjunctive contexts of terms and environments respectively while the stoup contains exactly one active formula - a term, context or command - at any given time.
They are read in the same direction as their elements.
On the type-level, we give a dialectical interpretation based on \cite{Herbelin2005CestMaintenant}, \cite{LovasCrary2006StructuralNormalization} and \cite{Harper2016PFPL}.

There are three judgments:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\Sigma\vdash v:\sigma \dashv \Delta$ judges $v$ to be a \emph{proof} for $\sigma$
\item $\Sigma\vdash \sigma:E \dashv \Delta$ judges $E$ to be a \emph{refutation} of $\sigma$
\item $\Sigma\vdash \langle v:\sigma:E\rangle \dashv \Delta$ judges $v$ and $E$ to form a \emph{contradiction} at $\sigma$
\end{enumerate}

The proof and refutation types of a given proposition are called \emph{contraries}.

In \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} commands are typed by a sequent with an empty stoup mirroring the usual cut rule.
However, we want to keep track of the issue of the contradiction and hence use the following typing rule:

\begin{center}

      \label{rule:S-ELIM}
      \symcut{v}{E}{\sigma}
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{minipage}
\end{center}

Instead of cutting the issue, the meeting of a contrary proof and refutation yields a contradiction.

\section{Constructing Arguments: the $ \Alambda$-calculus}

The \matilda calculus provides us with two of the three requirements we identified in \cref{sec:argum-debat-prop}: conflicting proof terms and control operators. The third feature - defeasibility - requires an extension of the language.

\subsection{Unwitnessed Premises: Obligations and Presumptions}

The notion of enthymemes traces back to Aristotle's rhetoric and is usually associated with two distinct kinds of ampliative inference steps (though the correct interpretation is disputed, see \cite{sep-aristotle-rhetoric}):

\begin{enumerate}
\item Deductive Arguments with missing premises (type-I-enthymemes);
\item Deductive Arguments with defeasible assumptions or inference steps (type-II-enthymemes).
\end{enumerate}

From a constructivist perspective both types of enthymemes contain subarguments that fail to furnish witnesses.
In this view the difference between the two consists merely in the burden-of-proof: type I enthymemes commit the proponent of the argument to present a witness if questioned; type II enthymemes instead commit any challenger to present a refuting witness.
To deal with both kinds of enthymemes we extend the grammar of \matilda terms and environments by two types of \emph{unwitnessed premises}: \emph{obligations} and \emph{presumptions}:

\begin{grammar}[Grammar of the $\matilda$-calculus][tbhp][gram:terms]
    \firstcasesubtil{v}{\dots \gralt [:\sigma] \gralt \underline{[:\sigma]} }{Term obligations and presumptions}
    \firstcasesubtil{E}{\dots \gralt [\sigma:]\gralt \underline{[\sigma:]}}{Environment obligations and presumptions}
\end{grammar}

\begin{notation}\label[notation]{not:enthymeme-nominals}
   We overload notation and use $[:\sigma]$ for the typing judgment $[:\sigma]:\sigma$ and $[\sigma:]$ for the typing judgment $\sigma:[\sigma:]$. In cases where typing information is irrelevant we write $[.:]$ for term side obligations and $[:.]$ for environment side obligations. We use these notations for presumptions as well, with the obvious substitutions.
 \end{notation}


Obligations are typed as follows:

\begin{center}
  \begin{minipage}{0.48\linewidth}
    \begin{prooftree}
      \label{rule:termenth}
      \infer0{\Sigma\vdash [:\sigma] \dashv \Delta}
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{minipage}

\begin{minipage}{0.48\linewidth}
    \begin{prooftree}
      \label{rule:contextenth}
      \infer0{\Sigma\vdash [\sigma:] \dashv \Delta}
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{minipage}
\end{center}

Note that obligations are trivializing: one can find an argument for any proposition so long as one retains the obligations to prove one's assumptions.
On the other hand, presumptions do not have introduction rules: what presumptions are permitted in a given situation world knowledge. Examples of such knowledge are general burden-of-proof delegations such as negation-by-failure, or the assertion of looping proof obligations in coinductive inference as well as concrete default knowledge such as ``birds can usually fly''.\ednote{MR: work out these examples. \subsection{Arguments}

Our definition of arguments is inspired by deductive argumentation \cite{besnard-review-argum} which views arguments as conditional proofs of the form $\Sigma \vdash_{\mathcal{L}} \varphi$ where the \emph{assumptions} $\Sigma$ and the \emph{conclusion} $\varphi$ are formulae of a given base logic ith consequence relation $\vdash_{\mathcal{L}}$.
`In sequent-based variant \cite{TODO},\ednote{TODO: sequent-based argumentation citation.} they then become single conclusion sequents.
However, we depart from this approach in two ways:
Firstly, unlike deductive argumentation, we do not wish to abstract away from the interior structure of the arguments.
Hence our approach is to define \emph{arguments} as ``open'' terms and \emph{counterarguments} as ``open'' environments.
Secondly, we want to avoid overloading object-language-level variables whose main functionality is hypothetical reasoning.
Unlike premises in conditonal proofs, argument assumptions are not merely hypothetical but closer to (defeasible) knowledge items.
Hence their main route of elimination is not discharge but defeat.

This introduces two distinctions from the standard deductive argumentation approach: firstly, in our setting arguments only depend on the assumptions which they use.
In particular, irrespective of the antecedent (succedent) it is not possible to attack an argument on an assumption that does not appear free in it.
Secondly, the same sequent represents many different arguments.
Instead our way of identifying arguments will function via defining a normal form.
The basic idea is that an argument is an \AC term where every subargument exposes a corresponding obligation which can be the target of debate operations such as attack or support.
This exposure is \emph{bureaucratic} in nature in that it itself has no logical content.

\begin{definition}[Bureaucratic Expressions]
  An \AC expression is a \emph{bureaucratic obligation} if it belongs to the following subgrammar:
  
  \begin{grammar}[Bureaucratic Obligation Expressions][][gram:bureau]
    \firstcasesubtil{$b$}{[:\sigma]}{}
       \otherform{[\sigma:]}{}
       \otherform{\mu \sigma:\alpha.\rangle b :\sigma: E \langle}{}
       \otherform{\mu \sigma:\alpha.\rangle v :\sigma: b \langle}{}
       \otherform{\rangle b :\sigma: E \langle.x : \sigma \mu}{}
       \otherform{\rangle v :\sigma: b \langle. x : \sigma \mu}{}
  \end{grammar}
\end{definition}

Bureaucratic obligation expressions make no logical progress but instead manage the control flow around obligations.

\begin{example}[Bureaucratic Obligation]
  Consider the expression $\mu \alpha:A \rightarrow B.\rangle \mu\_.\rangle \lambda x. [:B] :: \alpha \langle :A\rightarrow B: [A \rightarrow B:]\langle$. Via one application of $\mu <$ it reducesto $\mu \alpha:A.\rangle \lambda x.[:B]:: \alpha \langle$ which is $\eta$-equivalent to $\lambda x.[:B]$. At the same time it exposes an obligation $[:A \rightarrow B]$ which could be filled in - for example - by a continuation that rerouts execution to use some alternative proof: $\rangle v' :A\rightarrow B: \alpha\langle.\_\mu$. In other words, we can think of an arbitrary proof term as a sceleton of obligations, fleshed out by an arbitrary number of proofs (refutations) for each sub-obligation.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Bureaucratic Obligation Redexes]
  Let $v,E$ be terms of the shapes  $\mu \alpha.\rangle \mu \_.\rangle v::\alpha\langle :: [.:] \langle$ and $ \rangle [:.] :: \rangle x:: E\langle .\_\mu\langle .x\mu$, respectively. A  \emph{bureaucratic obligation redex} is one of the following expressions:
  \[
    \mu \sigma:\alpha.\rangle  v :\sigma: E' \langle
  \]
  \[
    \rangle v' :\sigma: E \langle.x : \sigma \mu
  \]
  \[
    \rangle v :\sigma: [\sigma:] \langle :\sigma: E' \langle.x: \sigma \mu
  \]
  \[
    \mu \sigma:\alpha.\rangle v' :\sigma: E \langle
  \]
  In each case, the bound command is a default redex; hence the whole surrounding $\mu$- or $\mu'$-expression is also a redex, and moreover bureaucratic.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[obligation-$\eta$ long form]
  An expression is in \emph{obligation-$\eta$ long form} iff obligation-$\eta$-expanding any of its subterms would introduce a bureaucratic obligation redex.
\end{definition}

With these ingredients in place we are finally ready to define our notion of argument:

\begin{definition}[Argument]
  Given a proposition $\sigma$ the \emph{arguments for $\sigma$} are the intuitionistic, obligation-$\eta$-long $\AC$-expressions $v:\sigma$. The \emph{arguments against $\sigma$} (or counterarguments to $\sigma$) are the dual-intuitionistic obligation-$\eta$-long $AC$ expressions $\sigma:E$. An \emph{argument} is either an argument for or against some type $t'$.
\end{definition}

      \end{prooftree}
    \end{minipage}
    \begin{minipage}{0.48\linewidth}
      \centering
      \begin{prooftree}
        \label{rule:S-ELIM}
      \symcut{v}{E}{\varphi}
    \end{prooftree}
    \end{minipage}
  \end{center}

\begin{center}

      \hypo{\Sigma \vdash \varphi:E \dashv \Delta}
      \infer1{\Sigma \vdash \text{not }E:\neg\varphi \dashv \Delta}
    \end{prooftree}

\subsection{Encoding Rebut}

We will do so making use of \emph{affine} and \emph{semi-affine} variables.
A $\mu$-bound variable is affine if it does not appear in the command in which it is bound.
It is semi-affine if it only appears on one side of of the command in which it is bound.
Affine variables are either terms that are thrown away or environments that are never passed to.
Exploiting this, affine uses of control operators can direct a term (environment) to a different bound variable.
Semi-affine variables on the other hand are only used in either the call-by-name or call-by-value discipline of execution.
This makes it possible to redirect a term (environment) to a different bound variable conditional on the evaluation order.
In particular, here we define a sentinel variable $\star$ which denotes an error term (environment):

% \begin{align*}
%   rebut_{left}&(t_1,t_2)= \
%   &\mu *:\sigma.\rangle \mu \_ :\sigma.\rangle t_1: \sigma : * \langle : \sigma : \sigma.\rangle\star:\sigma t_2:\langle .\sigma:\star\mu\langle
% \end{align*}

%\[rebut_{left}(t_1,t_2)= \mu *:\sigma.\rangle \mu \_ :\sigma.\rangle t_1: \sigma :* \langle : \sigma : \rangle\star:\sigma : t_2\langle .\sigma:\_\mu\langle\]
\[rebut_{left}(t_1,t_2)= \mu *:\sigma.\rangle \mu \_ :\sigma.\rangle t_1: \sigma :* \langle : \sigma : \rangle\star:\sigma : t_2\langle .\sigma:\_\mu\langle\]

The reserved environment variable $*$ indicates that the rest of the term is to be evaluated in call-by-value discipline.
Then the term is evaluated in one step as follows:

\begin{align}
  \label{eq:1}
  &\mu *:\sigma.\rangle t_1: \sigma :* \langle & & (\mu<)
\end{align}

Now $*$ becomes fully affine and thus the entire outer context gets thrown away if the expression is ever evaluated. Instead the sentinel value $\star$ gets passd to $t_2$.
The proof can now only be completed if a new proof of $\sigma$ is supplied.

In other words, (left) rebut turns a proof into an \emph{argument} predicated on the default assumption, that the counterargument can be defeated in some way.
