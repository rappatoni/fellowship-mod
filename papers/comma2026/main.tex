\title{Representing Arguments and Debates as Expressions of Classical Type Theory}
\runningtitle{Representing Arguments and Debates as Expressions of Classical Type Theory}

\author[A]{\fnms{Max} \snm{Rapp}}
\runningauthor{M. Rapp}
\address[A]{FAU Erlangen-Nuremberg}

\begin{abstract}
  We present a representation of arguments and debates as expressions of the \matilda-calculus furnishing argument and debate objects with computational content: successful arguments return normally, defeated ones return an exception. Arguments and counterarguments are constructed in the linear, intuitionistic fragment of the calculus. Debates combine them into classical expressions. We show how such a representation handles conflicting terms, control flow manipulation, argument relations combining arguments to debates. The encoding makes use of throw, catch and keep constructions that mirror exception handling to capture attack and support between arguments. The resulting \ACDC sublanguage allows a debate for Peirce's law via a term that normalizes to a \matilda proof and is hence complete with respect to the original calculus.
\end{abstract}

  \paragraph{Conflicts:} Prover asserts $((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$ but Skeptic denies this. Following \cite{KrauseAmblerElvangGoranssonFox1995}'s approach, we could represent each argument as a term $p:((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$ and $s:\neg((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$, respectively. But how are we to construct a term $d = ?(p,s):((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$ that suitable combines argument and counterargument into a debate about $((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$?
  \paragraph{Control Flow:} Throughout our example Prover and Skeptic challenge each other to prove subgoals necessary for the continuation of the debate. However, each side can choose which claim it argues at any given time: in particular, Prover sets a trap for Skeptic and when she walks into it by assuming $P$, changes the topic back to an earlier challenge and points out that Skeptic has contradicted herself. Such topic changes and argumentative maneuvering are par for the course in \emph{concrete} debates. If we wish to represent them we need a way to manipulate the \emph{control flow} of debate terms.
  % Suppose now we had a debate term $d:((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$. Either the proposition or the opposition in a debate usually \emph{wins}. Presumably, if the proposition wins, evaluating $d$ should yield a witness for $((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$. But if the opposition wins, $d$ should return a value that indicates the proposition's failure to make its case.
  \paragraph{Compilation:} Prover eventually ends the debate by closing all of Skeptic's possible response avenues (corresponding to a classical proof). In general, however, the use of implicit assumptions or defeasible defaults in arguments means that a given debate can always continue or - conversely - is never incomplete. Hence we should be able to represent the debate between Prover and Skeptic at different times and obtain debate terms $d_1, \dots, d_n$ that are built incrementally from each other. That is, we need compilation operations that allow us to construct debate terms from the arguments and counterarguments currently in the context.

  If the system's terms cannot represent the interaction of arguments in debates in the same way it captures individual arguments, the generic tool support we are hoping for is beyond our reach.
  Thus we need to adopt a richer type theory that can handle the phenomena of conflict, control flow and compilation and represent both individual arguments and debates in a unified language.
  In this paper we focus on the former two aspects, leaving (interactive) compilation for future work.

\section{Classical Type Theory, Dialectics and Control}\label{sec:class-type-theory}

  \cite{Griffin1990-FormulaeAsTypesOfControl, Parigot1992-LambdaMu, Wadler2003-CBVIsDualToCBN} and \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} extend the proposition-as-types correspondence to classical logic. Griffin showed that \emph{control operators} such as call-cc can be typed by Peirce's law; Parigot's $\lambda\mu$-calculus provides the Curry-Howard-Correspondent to classical natural deduction; \cite{Wadler2003-CBVIsDualToCBN}, \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} invented symmetric systems, in the latter case the \matilda calculus which is to the classical sequent calculus LK as $\lambda\mu$-calculus is to classical natural deduction.

  The latter system can be transposed into a dialectical natural deduction system \cite{Herbelin2005CestMaintenant}, \cite{LovasCrary2006StructuralNormalization} \cite{Harper2016PFPL} of \emph{proofs and refutations}, providing us with the second one of our requirements.

\subsection{The $\matilda$ Calculus - Dialectical Variant}\label{sec:matilda-calc-dial}

We recap the $\matilda$ Calculus and give a dialectical variant of its syntax.
\paragraph{Expressions} are divided into terms, environments and commands.
Terms are read left-to-right while environments are read right-to-left.
Commands are read either left-to-right or right-to-left depending on how they are bound.
The terminology on the expression level gestures at an interpretation of $\matilda$-expressions as program terms and continuation contexts.
Terms are expressions with outer holes that wait to be passed to a context.
Contexts are expressions with inner holes that await a term to be passed to them.
Commands assign terms to contexts.
The $\mu$ binder when binding to the right represents a call-with-current-continuation.
Symmetrically, when binding to the left $\mu$ corresponds to a call-with-current-expression.

  \begin{grammar}[Grammar of the $\matilda$-calculus][tbhp][gram:terms]
    \firstcasesubtil{$v$}{x \gralt \lambda x:\sigma.v \gralt E \circ v \gralt \mu \sigma:\alpha.c}{Terms}

    \firstcasesubtil{$E$}{\alpha \gralt E.\sigma:\alpha\lambda \gralt v \circ E \gralt c.x:\sigma \mu}{Environments}

    \firstcasesubtil{$c$}{\rangle v :\sigma: E \langle}{Commands}

    \firstcasesubtil{$\sigma,\delta$}{a \gralt \sigma \rightarrow \delta \gralt \sigma - \delta}{Propositions}
  \end{grammar}

We say that a $\mu$-bound variable is \emph{affine}  if it does not appear free in the command in which it is bound (likewise we call a binder binding an affine variable an \emph{affine binder}).
If we want to explicitly indicate that a binder is affine we write $\mu\_.c$ and $c.\_\mu$, respectively.
Two fragments of the syntax are of interest:
\emph{Intuitionistic Logic} is obtained by restricting the domain of the environment-variables $\alpha$ to a singleton set.
Conversely, \emph{Dual-intuitionistic Logic} is obtained by restricting the term variable domain to a singleton.
The intuitionistic and dual-intuitionistic fragments can alternatively be obtained by restricting expressions to \emph{linear} terms and contexts:

\begin{definition}[Linear Expressions]
  A variable $x$ is \emph{linearly bound} in an expression $t$ with outer binder $b$ iff $x$ is bound by $b$ in $t$, appears exactly once in $t$ and between $b$ and $x$ there is no binder occurence of the same kind as $b$. A \matilda expression is called \emph{linear} iff all term variables bound in it are bound linearly. It is called \emph{dual-linear} if all context variables bound in it are bound linearly.
\end{definition}
The intuitionistic fragment corresponds to the sublanguage of linear \matilda expressions, the dual-intuitionistic fragment to the sublanguage of dual-linear \matilda expressions.

\paragraph{Judgments and Typing:} Sequents have the form \emph{Antecedent $\vdash$ Stoup $\dashv$ Succedent}.
Antecedent and Succedent are conjunctive contexts of terms and environments respectively while the stoup contains exactly one active formula - a term, context or command - at any given time.
They are read in the same direction as their elements.
On the type-level, we give a dialectical interpretation based on \cite{Herbelin2005CestMaintenant}, \cite{LovasCrary2006StructuralNormalization} and \cite{Harper2016PFPL}.

There are three judgments:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\Sigma\vdash v:\sigma \dashv \Delta$ judges $v$ to be a \emph{proof} for $\sigma$
\item $\Sigma\vdash \sigma:E \dashv \Delta$ judges $E$ to be a \emph{refutation} of $\sigma$
\item $\Sigma\vdash \langle v:\sigma:E\rangle \dashv \Delta$ judges $v$ and $E$ to form a \emph{contradiction} at $\sigma$
\end{enumerate}

Terms are typed by proofs, contexts by refutations and commands by contradictions.
Computationally, proofs type terms that produce a witness for their proposition regardless of input.
Refutations type contexts that halt computation upon being passed a proof witness for their proposition, allowing for arbitrary returns.
Contradictions type commands that can return arbitrary outputs from arbitrary inputs.
The proof and refutation types of a given proposition are called \emph{contraries}.

In \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} commands are typed by a sequent with an empty stoup mirroring the usual cut rule.
However, we want to keep track of the issue of the contradiction and hence use the following typing rule:

\begin{center}

\end{align*}

\subsection{Control Flow: Affine Binders, Throw,  Keep and Catch}\label{sec:control-flow:-affine}

The reduction system brings to the fore how control flow is modelled in the $\matilda$-calculus.
Of particular interest to us is the reduction behavior of affine terms. Note that affine $\mu$ binders throw away their contexts (terms) during reduction:

\[\mu \beta.\rangle \mu\_.\rangle v :: E \langle :: F[\beta] \langle \longrightarrow_{\mu <} \mu \beta.\rangle v :: E \langle\]

Further, so long as $\beta$ does not appear free in $v,E$, the outer $\mu \beta$ becomes affine itself. In this way the inner command ``bubbles up'' mirroring the behavior of \emph{exceptions} in common programming languages.
For this reason, classical type theory has long been used as a setting to study exception handling (see \cite{vanBakel2019ExceptionHandling}) for an overview.
However, the target calculus is usually Parigot's $\lambda\mu$-calculus .
We adopt some of the notions developed in that context to the $\matilda$-calculus.
For present purposes, only the following minimal account is necessary, a full treatment is left of future work.
We define the following subgrammar of affine expressions :

\begin{grammar}[Throw and Keep][][gram:throw]
  \firstcasesubtil{throw}{\mu \sigma :\_.\rangle v:\sigma:\alpha\langle \gralt \rangle x:\sigma:E\langle.\_:\sigma\mu}{Throw Terms}
  \firstcasesubtil{keep}{\mu \sigma :\_.\rangle x:\sigma:E\langle \gralt \rangle v:\sigma:\alpha\langle.\_:\sigma\mu}{Keep Terms}
\end{grammar}

\begin{notation}[Throw/Keep]
  Throw and keep terms are abbreviated as $throw(v,:\alpha)$ and $(x:,E)keep$, respectively; throw and keep contexts as $(x:,E)throw$ and $keep(v, :\alpha)$, respectively.
\end{notation}

In the throw case, the current continuation is trashed and the current term $v$ (context $E$) is thrown to an outer catch continuation (term). The pathological command will continue to bubble up until it gets \emph{caught} by a binder that binds some variable free in the exception command $c$. This mirrors the computational behaviour of exception handling, where $v$ ($E$) is the pathological term (context) to be handled. However, unlike in the usual treatment of exceptions - the exception $v$ is not thrown to any particular catch continuation but can in principle be catchable on (any number of) term or context variables occuring free in it.

Thus any $\mu$-abstracted term (environment) \ednote{Unify term/environment/context/continuation terminology} can catch a thrown term.

\begin{notation}[Catch]
  We write $catch(:\alpha)$ and $(x:)catch$, for term (context) side $\mu$ binders, respectively, if we with to stress their role as a catch statement.
\end{notation}

In the keep case, the current term (continuation) is trashed and an inner term $v$ (continuation $E$) is returned to the current continuation $\alpha$ (term $x$). This behaviour can be thought of as an access-restricted computation that checks the continuation before returning to it and if it does not have access rights to the current value, returns another value instead.

Keep terms allow the modelling of total failure or abortion of a computation, as the following example demonstrates:

\begin{example}[Contradictory Proof terms]\label{ex:control-flow:-affine-1}
  Assume a atoms $t:A$ and $A:t'$. Consider the following proof term:

  \[\mu A:\alpha.\rangle t:: \rangle \mu\_.\rangle x:: t' \langle:: \alpha\langle.x:A\mu\langle:A\]

  Given that we have a contradiction, simply returning $t$ to $\alpha$ would hide the pathology. Instead, the contradiction should be brought to the surface. The continuation $ \rangle \mu\_.\rangle x:: t' \langle:: \alpha\langle.x:A\mu$ thus denies $t$ access to the outer continuation $\alpha$ and instead reroutes it to the abort continuation $t'$. The term hence reduces to
  $$ \mu A:\alpha.\rangle t::t'\langle \text{,}$$ bringing the contradiction to the top and yielding an uncatchable exception that will consume any continuation to which it is passed - in other words \emph{aborting} the computation.
  
\end{example}

\section{Modelling Arguments and their Relations in the dialectical \matilda-calculus}\label{sec:modell-argum-relat}

We generalize the approach taken by \cite{KrauseAmblerElvangGoranssonFox1995} to define arguments in the \matilda-setting.
The basic idea is that a successful argument should correspond to a program that returns normally, while a defeated argument should abort the computation.

\begin{definition}[Arguments]
  We call any linear term $t:\sigma$ an \emph{argument for} $\sigma$. A dual-linear context $\sigma:t$ is called an \emph{argument against} $\sigma$ or \emph{counterargument to} $\sigma$. An \emph{argument} is either an argument for or against some proposition $\sigma$. Arguments are not required to be closed expressions: the free variables in an argument $t$ are called the \emph{assumptions} of t.
\end{definition}

We use $t[t']$ to indicate that an expression $t'$ is an expression $t$. For variables we take $t[x], t[\alpha]$ to mean that $x,\alpha$ occur free in $t$. 

 Two design choices require justification: firstly, why are (counter-)arguments restricted to the (dual-)intuitionistic fragment? Secondly, why use free variables as an argument's assumptions?

\paragraph{Arguments and Control Flow:} The motivation for the (dual-)intuitionistic restriction is twofold: from a logical perspective the divide between constructive intuitionistic proofs and non-constructive classical proofs - i.e. demonstrations, that the given proposition cannot be refuted - map congenially to a corresponding dialectical distinction between concrete arguments for (against) a given proposition and debates about a proposition where the proponent (opponent) has a winning strategy.
Computationally speaking, the move to the classical calculus introduces precisely the non-linearity (e.g. traps or topic changes) that distinguishes single-agent, sequential reasoning from multi-agent, adversarial reasoning.

\paragraph{Assumptions: } here are three broad approaches in the literature: assumptions as meta-level default-assumptions or inference rules (e.g. \cite{BondarenkoDungKowalskiToni1997DefaultReasoning}, ); assumptions as object logic axioms (e.g. \cite{BesnardHunter2001DeductiveArguments}) and assumptions as proof calculus context variables (e.g.\cite{ArieliStrasser2015SequentBasedLogicalArgumentation}).
While we certainly regard a dedicated treatment of assumptions as desirable we leave this to future work and stay close to the latter approach - if only for its simplicity.
However, we make our notion of arguments as concrete expresions instead of abstract sequents has the advantage that attacks/supports can only target assumptions that are actually used by the targeted argument.

\subsection{Argument Relations in the \matilda-calculus}

In this section we will put the the throw, keep and catch patterns we developed in \cref{sec:control-flow:-affine} to work to model the argumentative relations of attack (rebut and undermine) and support (buttress and undergird).

\paragraph{Support:} The basic idea is that we can use throw-keep-catch patterns to model alternative arguments for a proposition.
This approach follows \cite{AutexierSacerdotiCoen2006OMDocLambdaMuMu}, who develop a pattern that allows the encoding of alternative proofs in \matilda-terms:

\[alt(t_1,t_2) = \mu \alpha.\rangle \mu\_.\rangle t_1 :: \alpha \langle :: \rangle t_2 :: \alpha \langle.\_\mu\langle\]

Using the concepts developed in \cref{sec:control-flow:-affine}, alternative proofs (refutations) can be viewed as the \emph{throw-keep-catch patterns}.
From an argumentative perspective, one can think of these patterns as \emph{support} between arguments.


% \begin{notation}[Throw/Keep/Catch Operators]
%   We write $\throwt{v}{:\alpha}$ and $\keept{v}{:\alpha}$ for term-side throw and keep, and $(x:,E)\operatorname{throw}$ and $(x:,E)\operatorname{keep}$ for their context-side duals. \begin{definition}[Support]
  Support is defined via the following grammar:
  \begin{grammar}[Throw-Keep-Catch Patterns][][gram:throwkeepcatch]
  \firstcasesubtil{$t_1\prec_\phi t_2$}{\catcht{: \alpha}.\rangle \throwt{t_1}{: \alpha} :\phi: \keept{t_2}{: \alpha} \langle}{Term-side pattern}
  \otherform{\rangle \keepc{x:}{t_1} :\phi: \throwc{x:}{t_2} \langle . \catchc{x:}}{Context-side pattern}
\end{grammar}
  An argument $t_1$ \emph{supports} an argument $t_2$ iff $t_2\prec t_1$. We say that $t_1$ \emph{undergirds} $t_2$ iff $t_2$ is a variable. Else $t_1$ \emph{buttresses} $t_2$. We define $\prec$ to be right-associative.
\end{definition}

\end{example}

\section{Debates in the \matilda-calculus: The \ACDC Sublanguage}\label{sec:debat-matilda-calc}

We now put the ingredients from the previous sections together to define a sublanguage that can represent debates including both support and attacks between arguments (and subdebates).

\begin{definition}[\ACDC Debate Terms]
  Let $v_l,E_l$ range over the linear \matilda expressions. Then \ACDC\ debate terms are given by the following subgrammar:
  \begin{grammar}[Debates][H]
    \firstcasesubtil{d}{v_l \gralt E_l \gralt d \prec_\sigma d' \gralt d \leftattack_\sigma d'}{}
  \end{grammar}
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Debate Term for Peirce's Law]
  From the arguments given in the previous examples, we can construct the following debate term:
  \[(P_1[x \prec S_1 [y \prec (P_2 [z \prec S_2 [u \prec P_3/u]/z]) [\zeta \prec S_3 [\iota / \prec P_4/\iota]/\zeta]/y]]/x)\leftattack S_1'\]
\end{example}

\subsection{Evaluating Debates:  Redundancy and Defeat}\label{sec:eval-debat-redund}

In order to flesh out our idea that argument and debate computations should return differently based on their acceptance status, we investigate the evaluation properties of debates.
When evaluating support patterns, one might decide to keep all supporters or only a subset of strongest supporters, in particular if some supporters are redundant.
We leave a full investigation of this to future work, here we only require a simple notion of redundancy based on strictness:

\begin{definition}[Strictness]
  Let $d,d'$ be debates such that $d[d']$. We say that $d'$ is \emph{strict} in $d$ iff every free variable in $d'$ is bound in $d$.
\end{definition}

Some care is required to account for caught variables: an argument cannot be discarded if it is used for a proof-by-contradiction of the contrary of one of its assumptions:

\begin{definition}[Strict Redundancy]
  Let $d,d_1,d_2$ be debates such that $d_1\leftattack_\phi d_2$ is a sub-expression of $d$. Then $d_2$ is \emph{strictly redundant} with respect to $d_1$ in $d$ iff $d_1$ is strict in $d$ and $d_2$ is either not strict in $d$ or a proof (refutation).
\end{definition}

In the case of attack patterns, the defeated alternative should be discarded. Like before, we define only a notion of strict defeat:

\begin{definition}[Strict Defeat]
  Let $d, d_1, d_2$ be debates such that $d_1,d_2$ are sub-expressions of $d$ and let $d_1\leftattack_\phi d_2$. Then $d_2$ \emph{strictly defeats} $d_1$ iff $d_2$ is a proof and $d_1$ is neither a proof nor strict in $d$
\end{definition}

Thus even proofs can be strictly defeated in inconsistent theories. Finally, we define the following partial evaluation strategy based on strict defeat and redundancy:

\begin{definition}[Call-By-Onus]
  Given a debate $d_1\leftattack_\phi d_2$ with command $c$, $d_1:\phi$ ($\phi:d_1$)and $\phi:d_2$ ($d_2:\phi$), such that $d_2$ strictly defeats $d_1$, apply $\mu <$ ($> \mu$). Else if $d_2$ is in normal form, apply $> \mu$ ($ \mu <$).  Else delay evaluation of $d_1\leftattack_\phi d_2$ and continue by evaluating $d_2$.
  Likewise, given a debate $d_1\prec_\phi d_2$ with command $c$, $d_1:\phi$ ($\phi:d_1$)and $\phi:d_2$ ($d_2:\phi$), such that $d_2$ is strictly redundant with respect to $d_1$, apply $\mu <$ ($> \mu$).  Else if $d_1$ is in normal form, apply $> \mu$ ($\mu <$). Else delay evaluation of $d_1\prec_\phi d_2$ and continue by evaluating $d_1$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Normalizing the Debate for Peirce's Law]
  Consider again the debate
  \[(P_1[x \prec S_1 [y \prec (P_2 [z \prec S_2 [u \prec P_3/u]/z]) [\zeta \prec S_3 [\iota / \prec P_4/\iota]/\zeta]/y]]/x)\leftattack S_1'.\]  
  We outline how the term is evaluated omitting non call-by-onus stepts. Initiall, we have to delay evaluation and reduce $S_1$ first. This yields an open term which is hence discarded via $\mu<$. We then have to delay evalation two times until we find $\iota$ to be an open term which is discarded via $\mu <$. Next we evaluate the expression $(P_2 [z \prec S_2 [u \prec P_3/u]/z]) \prec S_3[\zeta \prec P_4/\zeta]]$  While $S_3[\zeta \prec P_4/\zeta]$ is strict, we still have to check if $P_2 [z \prec S_2 [u \prec P_3/u]/z]) $ is strict as well. Indeed it is because $P_3$ is strict, rendering $S_2$ and then $P_2$ strict. Hence $S_3[\zeta \prec P_4/\zeta]$ is discarded and the final term is:

  \[P_1[[S_1[P_2[S_2[P_3/u]/z]/y]/x]\]

which in corresponds to the term $\mu \alpha.\rangle \lambda f.\mu \beta. \rangle f :: \lambda x.\rangle x :: \beta \langle\circ\beta\langle:: \alpha\langle$


\end{example}

\subsection{Classicality of  \ACDC}

Finally, we would like to know if we can recover the full logical expressivity of \matilda in \ACDC.
For our purposes, this will be the case if we can obtain a proof for every classical tautology by normalizing an \ACDC debate:

\begin{definition}[Reduction-closed Fragment]
  The \emph{$\mathcal{S}$-reduction-closed} fragment of \ACDC is the set of \ACDC terms that can be reduced via some reduction strategy $\mathcal{S}$ to a closed \matilda expression.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Soundness of \ACDC]
  The reduction-closed fragment of \ACDC is sound with respect to classical propositional logic.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  Immediate from the fact that closed \ACDC terms are a sublanguage of the closed $\matilda$-terms.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Completeness of \ACDC]
  The reduction-closed fragment of \ACDC is complete with respect to classical propositional logic.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  This follows from the fact that intuitionistic logic + Peirce's law a complete proof system for classical logic and that all intuitionistic terms are atomic \ACDC debates: let $T$ be a propositional tautology. There exists an intuitionistic argument $t_0:T$ with exactly one free variable $x:((\sigma\rightarrow \delta)\rightarrow \sigma) \rightarrow \sigma]$ for some intuitionistic formulae $\sigma, \delta$. We construct a debate $t_1$ for Peirce's law as we did in the previous section. Then $t_2= t_0[x \prec_{((\sigma\rightarrow \delta)\rightarrow \sigma) \rightarrow \sigma} t_1/x]$ is a debate for $T$. As x is not strict in $t_2$, there exists a reduction that reduces $t_2$ via call-by-onus to a term $t_0[t_1]$. As $t_1$ can be reduced to a closed term for Peirce's law, $t_0$ can be reduced to a closed term $t_0*:T$.  
\end{proof}

\section{Future Work and Conclusion}\label{sec:future-work-concl}

We have introduced a novel approach to represent arguments and debates as \matilda terms furnishing argument and debate objects with computational content such that successful arguments for a given issue return normally whereas defeated arguments return an exception.
Arguments are conceived as linear, potentially open \matilda terms and debates compose arguments via the support and attack syntactic patterns.
In case of support, either the original argument or its supporter is returned, based on whether the supporter is considered redundant.
As for attacks, a successful attack results in an uncatchable exception that aborts the defeated arguments computation.
The resulting \ACDC sublanguage is classical as it allows a debate for Peirce's law via a term that normalizes to a \matilda proof.
In future work we will investigate open debates, i.e. debates that do not normalize to a closed \matilda-term in terms of Dungean argumentation semantics.
We will also present a fully general reduction strategies for \ACDC terms and extend the syntax of the language to include genuine enthymemes replacing the use of free variables.
This will be the base for interactive construction of arguments and debates. 
Further research directions include correspondences between \ACDC debate terms and Lorenzen game winning strategies, a first order extension of the language and its use as a logical framework in the style of \emph{Isabelle}.
