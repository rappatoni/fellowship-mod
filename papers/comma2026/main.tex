  \paragraph{Conflicts:} Prover asserts $((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$ but Skeptic denies this. Following \cite{Ambler}'s approach, we could represent each argument as a term $p:((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$ and $s:\neg((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$, respectively. But how are we to construct a term $d = ?(p,s):((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$ that suitable combines argument and counterargument into a debate about $((P\rightarrow Q)\rightarrow P)\rightarrow P$?
  \paragraph{Control Flow:} Throughout our example Prover and Skeptic challenge each other to prove subgoals necessary for the continuation of the debate. However, each side can choose which claim it argues at any given time: in particular, Prover sets a trap for Skeptic and when she walks into it by assuming $P$, changes the topic back to an earlier challenge and points out that Skeptic has contradicted herself. Such topic changes and argumentative maneuvering are par for the course in \emph{concrete} debates. If we wish to represent them we need a way to manipulate the \emph{control flow} of debate terms.

\section{The $\matilda$ Calculus - Dialectical Variant}
  \cite{Griffin1990-FormulaeAsTypesOfControl, Parigot1992-LambdaMu, Wadler2003-CBVIsDualToCBN} and \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} extend the proposition-as-types correspondence to classical logic. Griffin showed that \emph{control operators} such as call-cc can be typed by Peirce's law; Parigot's $\lambda\mu$-calculus provides the Curry-Howard-Correspondent to classical natural deduction; \cite{Wadler2003-CBVIsDualToCBN}, \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} invented symmetric systems, in the latter case the \matilda calculus which is to the classical sequent calculus LK as $\lambda\mu$-calculus is to classical natural deduction.

  The latter system can be transposed into a dialectical natural deduction system \cite{Herbelin2005CestMaintenant}, \cite{LovasCrary2006StructuralNormalization} \cite{Harper2016PFPL} of \emph{proofs and refutations}, providing us with the second one of our requirements.

We recap the $\matilda$ Calculus and give a dialectical variant of its syntax.
Expressions are divided into terms, environments and commands.
Terms are read left-to-right while environments are read right-to-left.
Commands are read either left-to-right or right-to-left depending on how they are bound.
The terminology on the expression level gestures at an interpretation of $\matilda$-expressions as program terms and continuation contexts.
Terms are expressions with outer holes that wait to be passed to a context.
Contexts are expressions with inner holes that await a term to be passed to them.
Commands assign terms to contexts.
The $\mu$ binder when binding to the right represents a call-with-current-continuation.
Symmetrically, when binding to the left $\mu$ corresponds to a call-with-current-expression.
We say that a $\mu$-bound variable is \emph{affine}  if it does not appear free in the command in which it is bound (likewise we call a binder binding an affine variable an \emph{affine binder}).
If we want to explicitly indicate that a binder is affine we write $\mu\_.c$ and $c.\_\mu$, respectively.
Two fragments of the syntax are of interest:
\emph{Intuitionistic Logic} is obtained by restricting the domain of the environment-variables $\alpha$ to a singleton set. We use $\star$ for this unique continuation variable whenever we want to work in the intuitionistic fragment (denoted \matilda$_{LJ}$).
Conversely, \emph{Dual-intuitionistic Logic} is obtained by restricting the term variable domain to a singleton. We use $\cdot$ to denote this unique term variable whenever we are wokring in the dual-intuitionistic fragment (denoted \matilda$_{JL}$).


  \begin{grammar}[Grammar of the $\matilda$-calculus][tbhp][gram:terms]
    \firstcasesubtil{$v$}{x \gralt \lambda x:\sigma.v \gralt E \circ v \gralt \mu \sigma:\alpha.c}{Terms}

    \firstcasesubtil{$E$}{\alpha \gralt E.\sigma:\alpha\lambda \gralt v \circ E \gralt c.x:\sigma \mu}{Environments}

    \firstcasesubtil{$c$}{\rangle v :\sigma: E \langle}{Commands}

    \firstcasesubtil{$\sigma,\delta$}{a \gralt \sigma \rightarrow \delta \gralt \sigma - \delta}{Propositions}
  \end{grammar}

Sequents have the form \emph{Antecedent $\vdash$ Stoup $\dashv$ Succedent}.
Antecedent and Succedent are conjunctive contexts of terms and environments respectively while the stoup contains exactly one active formula - a term, context or command - at any given time.
They are read in the same direction as their elements.
On the type-level, we give a dialectical interpretation based on \cite{Herbelin2005CestMaintenant}, \cite{LovasCrary2006StructuralNormalization} and \cite{Harper2016PFPL}.

There are three judgments:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\Sigma\vdash v:\sigma \dashv \Delta$ judges $v$ to be a \emph{proof} for $\sigma$
\item $\Sigma\vdash \sigma:E \dashv \Delta$ judges $E$ to be a \emph{refutation} of $\sigma$
\item $\Sigma\vdash \langle v:\sigma:E\rangle \dashv \Delta$ judges $v$ and $E$ to form a \emph{contradiction} at $\sigma$
\end{enumerate}

Terms are typed by proofs, contexts by refutations and commands by contradictions.
Computationally, proofs type terms that produce a witness for their proposition regardless of input.
Refutations type contexts that halt computation upon being passed a proof witness for their proposition, allowing for arbitrary returns.
Contradictions type commands that can return arbitrary outputs from arbitrary inputs.
The proof and refutation types of a given proposition are called \emph{contraries}.

In \cite{CurienHerbelin2000-DualityOfComputation} commands are typed by a sequent with an empty stoup mirroring the usual cut rule.
However, we want to keep track of the issue of the contradiction and hence use the following typing rule:

\section{Arguments in the \matilda-calculus}
We generalize the approach taken by \cite{ambler} to define arguments in the \matilda-setting.

\begin{definition}[Arguments]
  We call any \matilda$_{LJ}$-term $t:\sigma$ an \emph{argument for} $\sigma$. A \matilda$_{JL}$ term $\sigma:t$ is called an \emph{argument against} $\sigma$ or \emph{counterargument to} $\sigma$. An \emph{argument} is either an argument for or against some proposition $\sigma$. Arguments are not required to be closed expressions: the free variables in an argument $t$ are called the \emph{assumptions} of t.
\end{definition}

Two design choices require justification: firstly, why are (counter-)arguments restricted to the (dual-)intuitionistic fragment? Secondly, why use free variables as an argument's assumptions?
The motivation for the (dual-)intuitionistic restriction is twofold: from a logical perspective the divide between constructive intuitionistic proofs and non-constructive classical proofs - i.e. demonstrations, that the given proposition cannot be refuted - map congenially to a corresponding dialectical distinction between concrete arguments for (against) a given proposition and debates about a proposition where the proponent (opponent) has a winning strategy.
Computationally speaking, the move to the classical calculus introduces precisely the non-linearity (e.g. traps or topic changes) that distinguishes single-agent, sequential reasoning from multi-agent, adversarial reasoning.
Regarding assumptions, there are three broad approaches in the literature: assumptions as meta-level default-assumptions or inference rules \cite{ABA}; assumptions as object logic axioms \cite{LBA}; and assumptions as proof calculus context variables \cite{sequent-based-argumentation}.
While we certainly regard a dedicated treatment of assumptions as desirable, we stay close to the latter approach - if only for its simplicity.
However, requiring that the assumptions of an argument appear in it (and not just in the proof context) has the advantage that attacks can only be made on assumptions that are actually used by the attacked argument.

\section{Control Flow: Affine Binders, Throw,  Keep and Catch}\label{sec:control-flow:-affine}

The reduction system brings to the fore how control flow is modelled in the $\matilda$-calculus.
Of particular interest to us is the reduction behavior of affine terms. Note that affine $\mu$ binders throw away their contexts (terms) during reduction:

\[\mu \beta.\rangle \mu\_.\rangle v :: E \langle :: F[\beta] \langle \longrightarrow_{\mu <} \mu \beta.\rangle v :: E \langle\]

Further, so long as $\beta$ does not appear free in $v,E$, the outer $\mu \beta$ becomes affine itself. In this way the inner command ``bubbles up'' mirroring the behavior of \emph{exceptions} in common programming languages.
For this reason, classical type theory has long been used as a setting to study exception handling \cite{old-literature}.
See \cite{van bakel} for an overview.
However, the target calculus is usually Parigot's $\lambda\mu$-calculus.
We adopt some of the notions developed in that context to the $\matilda$-calculus.
For present purposes, only the following minimal account is necessary, a full treatment is left of future work.
We define the following subgrammar of affine expressions :

\begin{grammar}[Throw and Keep][][gram:throw]
  \firstcasesubtil{throw}{\mu \sigma :\_.\rangle v:\sigma:\alpha\langle \gralt \rangle x:\sigma:E\langle.\_:\sigma\mu}{Throw Terms}
  \firstcasesubtil{keep}{\mu \sigma :\_.\rangle x:\sigma:E\langle \gralt \rangle v:\sigma:\alpha\langle.\_:\sigma\mu}{Keep Terms}
\end{grammar}

\begin{notation}[Throw/Keep]
  Throw and keep terms are abbreviated as $throw(v,:\alpha)$ and $(x:,E)keep$, respectively; throw and keep contexts as $(x:,E)throw$ and $keep(v, :\alpha)$, respectively.
\end{notation}

In the throw case, the current continuation is trashed and the current term $v$ (context $E$) is thrown to an outer catch continuation (term). The pathological command will continue to bubble up until it gets \emph{caught} by a binder that binds some variable free in the exception command $c$. This mirrors the computational behaviour of exception handling, where $v$ ($E$) is the pathological term (context) to be handled. However, unlike in the usual treatment of exceptions - the exception $v$ is not thrown to any particular catch continuation but can in principle be catchable on (any number of) term or context variables occuring free in it.

Thus any $\mu$-abstracted term (environment) \ednote{Unify term/environment/context/continuation terminology} can catch a thrown term.

\begin{notation}[Catch]
  We write $catch(:\alpha)$ and $(x:)catch$, for term (context) side $\mu$ binders, respectively, if we with to stress their role as a catch statement.
\end{notation}

In the keep case, the current term (continuation) is trashed and an inner term $v$ (continuation $E$) is returned to the current continuation $\alpha$ (term $x$). This behaviour can be thought of as an access-restricted computation that checks the continuation before returning to it and if it does not have access rights to the current value, returns another value instead.

Keep terms allow the modelling of total failure or abortion of a computation, as the following example demonstrates:

\begin{example}[Contradictory Proof terms]\label{ex:control-flow:-affine-1}
  Assume a atoms $t:A$ and $A:t'$. Consider the following proof term:

  \[\mu A:\alpha.\rangle t:: \rangle \mu\_.\rangle x:: t' \langle:: \alpha\langle.x:A\mu\langle:A\]

  Given that we have a contradiction, simply returning $t$ to $\alpha$ would hide the pathology. Instead, the contradiction should be brought to the surface. The continuation $ \rangle \mu\_.\rangle x:: t' \langle:: \alpha\langle.x:A\mu$ thus denies $t$ access to the outer continuation $\alpha$ and instead reroutes it to the abort continuation $t'$. The term hence reduces to
  $$ \mu A:\alpha.\rangle t::t'\langle \text{,}$$ bringing the contradiction to the top and yielding an uncatchable exception that will consume any continuation to which it is passed - in other words \emph{aborting} the computation.
  
\end{example}

\section{Modelling Argument Relations  in the dialectical \matilda-calculus}

In this section we will put the the throw, keep and catch patterns we developed in \cref{sec:control-flow:-affine} to work to model the argumentative relations of attack (rebut and undermine) and support (buttress and undergird).
The basic idea is that we can use throw-keep-catch patterns to model alternative arguments for a proposition following an approach pioneered by \cite{claudio-paper}.
Attack can then be encoded by giving two alternative arguments for the thesis: the original one and one from contradiction.

\subsection{Support}

\cite{claudio-paper} develop a pattern that allows the encoding of alternative proofs in \matilda-terms:

\[alt(t_1,t_2) = \mu \alpha.\rangle \mu\_.\rangle t_1 :: \alpha \langle :: \rangle t_2 :: \alpha \langle.\_\mu\langle\]

Using the concepts developed in \cref{sec:control-flow:-affine}, alternative proofs (refutations) can be viewed as the following \emph{throw-keep-catch patterns}:

\begin{grammar}[Throw-Keep-Catch Patterns][][gram:throwkeepcatch]
  \firstcasesubtil{$t_1\prec_\phi t_2$}{catch(:\alpha).\rangle throw(t_1,:\alpha) :\phi: keep(t_2, :\alpha)  \langle}{}
  \otherform{\rangle (x:,t_1)keep :\phi: (x:,t_2)throw\langle.(x:)catch}{}
\end{grammar}

We define $\prec$ to be right-associative.


From an argumentative perspective, one can think of these patterns as \emph{support} between arguments.\ednote{MR@gptel: please research the literature on the different notions of support here and compare to our notion of support.}


\begin{definition}[Support]
  An argument $t_1$ \emph{supports} an argument $t_2$ iff $t_2\succ t_1$ or $t_1 \prec t_2$. We say that $t_1$ \emph{undergirds} $t_2$ iff $t_2$ is a variable. Else $t_1$ \emph{buttresses} $t_2$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Attack}
Attack in structured argumentation is usually modelled via a notion of contrariness defined on top of the base logic.\cite{aba, sequent_based_arg}
We instead define contrariness bottom-up by treating proofs and refutations as contrary to each other.\footnote{Note that in the classical calculus contrariness defined in this way becomes classical negation. But this need not be so: in the minimal and intuitionistic fragment, the equivalence between the two notions does not hold.}

  \begin{definition}[Attack]
    Let $t_1,t_2$ be intuitonistic $\matilda$ expressions. An \emph{attack} between $_1$ and $t_2$ is defined as one of the following patterns:

    \begin{grammar}[Attacks][][gram:attacks]
      %\firstcasesubtil{attack term}{\mu \sigma:\alpha.\rangle \mu\_ .\rangle t_1: \sigma : \alpha \langle : \sigma : \rangle t_1 :\sigma : t_2 \langle .\_ \mu\langle}{Attack Terms}
      %\firstcasesubtil{attack context}{\rangle \mu \_ . \rangle t_1 :\sigma: t_2 \langle :\sigma: \rangle x :\sigma: t_2\langle. \_ \mu \langle. x:\sigma \mu}{Attack Contexts}
      \firstcasesubtil{attack term}{\mu \sigma:\alpha.\rangle t_1:: \rangle \mu\_.\rangle x:: t_2 \langle:: \rangle x::\alpha\langle.\_\mu\langle.x\mu\langle}{Attack Terms}

\begin{theorem}[Soundness of \ACDC]
  The closed fragment of \ACDC is sound with respect to classical propositional logic.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  Immediate from the fact that closed \ACDC terms are a sublanguage of the closes $\matilda$-terms
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Completeness of \ACDC]
  The closed fragment of \ACDC is complete with respect to classical propositional logic.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  This follows from the fact that intuitionistic logic + Peirce's law is complete wrt Boolean algebras and that all intuitionistic terms are atomic \ACDC debates: let $T$ be a propositional tautology. There exists an intuitionistic argument $t_0$ with exactly one obligation $[:((sigma\rightarrow \delta))\rightarrow \sigma \rightarrow \sigma]$ for some intuitionistic formulae $\sigma, \delta$. We construct a debate $t_1$ for Peirce's law as we did in the previous section. Then $support(t_2, t_1, t_0)$ is a debate for $T$. Via $\dots$ $t_2$ reduces to a term $t_0[t_1]$ and as $t_1$ reduces to a closed term for Peirce's law, $t_0$ reduces to a closed term $t_0*:T$.  
\end{proof}
